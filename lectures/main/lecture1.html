What is Digital Humanities?

Digital Humanities is not about replacing traditional methods—it's about expanding our toolkit for understanding human culture.

Traditional Humanities

  • Close reading of texts
  • Historical analysis
  • Cultural interpretation
  • Qualitative research
  • Narrative storytelling
  • Individual expertise

Digital Methods

  • Computational text analysis
  • Data visualization
  • Network analysis
  • Digital archives
  • Interactive narratives
  • Collaborative research

The goal: Use both approaches together to ask deeper questions about human experience.

4 / 23

Why Code in the Humanities?

Coding gives us superpowers for cultural analysis:

  • Scale: Analyze thousands of documents at once (imagine reading every 19th-century novel!)
  • Pattern Recognition: Discover hidden connections across time and space
  • Visualization: Make abstract concepts tangible and shareable
  • Preservation: Digitize and protect cultural heritage for future generations
  • Accessibility: Make knowledge more democratic and inclusive
  • New Questions: Ask what wasn't possible before technology
  • Collaboration: Work across disciplines and institutions
"We're not just using computers to do humanities faster—we're using them to do humanities differently."

Douglas Rushkoff: Capabilities

"[T]he underlying capability of the computer era is actually programming—which almost none of us know how to do. We simply use the programs that have been made for us, and enter our text in the appropriate box on the screen. We teach kids how to use software to write, but not how to write software. This means they have access to the capabilities given to them by others, but not the power to determine the value-creating capabilities of these technologies for themselves."
Douglas Rushkoff, qtd in Nick Montfort's Exploratory Programming for the Arts and Humanities, 327-28

Facebook's Gender Binaries

The example illustrates a broader challenge in platform design: technical systems often default to binary classifications for computational efficiency and advertiser convenience, even when the platform's stated values support inclusivity. These seemingly neutral coding decisions embed particular worldviews about gender into algorithmic systems that affect millions of users.

Questions for Reflection

Let's start thinking like digital humanists. Consider these questions:

  • Personal Interest: What aspects of culture or human experience intrigue you most?
